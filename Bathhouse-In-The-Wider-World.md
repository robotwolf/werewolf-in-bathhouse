# Werewolf Bathhouse

## Bathhouse In The Wider World

### Purpose
This document defines what the bathhouse is in civic, economic, and dramatic terms.

The goal is to make the bathhouse feel like a real institution under pressure, not just a spooky level with towels.

It also sharpens the mystery direction:

**The bathhouse is dealing with a personnel and liability disaster, not a shocking revelation that werewolves exist.**

### The Bathhouse's Actual Role
The bathhouse is a working-class fantasy wellness venue with one foot in hospitality and one foot in controlled hazard management.

It serves:

- laborers coming off ugly shifts
- lower-status magical citizens who are tolerated elsewhere but not welcomed
- regulars who want privacy without luxury pricing
- people who need treatment rooms, heat, or cleansing facilities tied to magical conditions
- discreet clients avoiding more respectable establishments

It is not the nicest place in the city.
It is useful.

That is more important.

### Why The Bathhouse Matters
The bathhouse should matter because it sits at the intersection of several systems at once:

- hygiene
- species accommodation
- gossip exchange
- side-business dealing
- labor networking
- low-level political management
- discreet vice

People come here to wash, recover, hide, negotiate, flirt, sulk, and quietly sort out problems that would look worse in a brighter district.

That makes it an ideal game space because every room can support:

- routine work
- social observation
- suspicious privacy
- practical evidence
- institutional stakes

### Why Management Cannot Simply Close
Closing for the night is costly and dangerous in ways that go beyond lost revenue.

If the bathhouse closes suddenly:

- regulars talk
- nearby rivals sniff weakness
- inspectors may ask why
- insurers may demand incident reporting
- staff lose confidence
- local fixers and suppliers get nervous
- the Boss looks unable to control his own floor

So management keeps trying to contain the situation inside ordinary operations for as long as possible. This is funny, tense, and exactly the sort of terrible decision a business under pressure would make.

### The Boss's Position
The Boss should be understood less as a quest-giver and more as a local power broker trying to prevent collapse.

The Boss cares about:

- keeping the doors open
- protecting the license
- avoiding a public death spiral
- maintaining authority over staff and regulars
- containing the central liability before outside institutions intervene

The Boss does not need to be noble. The Boss does need to be rational from their own perspective.

Good Boss energy:

- tired competence
- selective honesty
- practical menace
- institutional paranoia
- flashes of genuine care buried under business language

### The Central Liability
The current design works best when one figure serves as the social center of the run's trouble.

This person is:

- known to the bathhouse orbit
- tied to multiple NPCs in different ways
- dangerous because of behavior, not species
- the source of legal, financial, and social risk

This character may be:

- a reckless young werewolf with pack ties
- a semi-regular who thinks charm cancels consequences
- an errand-runner for worse people
- someone the Boss used to protect and now cannot safely cover for

The crucial point is that he makes everyone around him lie for different reasons.

That is the mystery engine.

### Why Everyone Acts Suspicious
The bathhouse works as a mystery location when suspicion has multiple sources.

People are evasive because they may be:

- covering up illegal work
- hiding sexual history
- ducking debt
- protecting a friend
- avoiding species prejudice
- concealing humiliating medical issues
- shielding themselves from management discipline
- scared of being linked to the liability case

This prevents the run from collapsing into a flat binary of innocent person versus monster. Instead, the player sees a building full of flawed adults with overlapping reasons to keep their mouths shut. Shocking stuff: cities are messy.

### Business Pressures That Drive The Plot
The bathhouse should always feel pressured by systems outside the walls.

#### License Risk
The business can be fined, suspended, or shut down if it is seen as unsafe, negligent, or unable to manage supernatural hazards.

#### Insurance Risk
Every injury, transformation incident, and unexplained property loss threatens future coverage and costs.

#### Reputation Risk
The bathhouse survives on repeat business and tolerated notoriety. Too much blood, scandal, or press attention tips tolerated notoriety into ruin.

#### Pack And Neighborhood Risk
If the wrong outsider steps in, the conflict stops being a private mess and becomes a public territorial dispute.

These pressures give the run a material timer without relying only on abstract apocalypse stakes.

### The Building's Social Geography
Different rooms should imply different forms of behavior and different kinds of lies.

#### Public Wet Zones
Pools, showers, steam rooms, and saunas.

Best for:

- overheard conversations
- nervous glances
- accidental sightings
- bodily evidence
- status performances

#### Semi-Private Transition Zones
Lockers, hallways, mirrors, benches, stairwells.

Best for:

- contradiction clues
- dropped items
- hurried confrontations
- avoidance behavior
- mistaken identity

#### Staff And Utility Zones
Laundry, boiler spaces, supply closets, staff corridors, service access.

Best for:

- hidden evidence
- management secrets
- maintenance gameplay
- proof of tampering
- signs that the bathhouse is barely under control

#### Special Or Restricted Rooms
Private rental rooms, treatment spaces, shrine niches, roof access, hidden undercroft links.

Best for:

- high-value clues
- secret meetings
- romantic or criminal entanglements
- revelations about the building's deeper history

### Bathhouse History
The bathhouse should feel old in layers rather than ancient in a single dramatic chunk.

A useful history stack:

1. The site had ritual or sacred use before the current city fully formed.
2. Industrial and civic expansion buried, repurposed, or built over that older use.
3. The current bathhouse emerged as a practical business over compromised infrastructure.
4. The modern operation inherited old energies, obligations, and layout weirdness it only partly understands.

This preserves room for a living-building or ritual-understructure thread without letting it eclipse the social mystery too early.

### What The Bathhouse Says About The City
The bathhouse should operate as a miniature of the broader setting.

Inside one building, the player sees:

- species coexistence under stress
- class difference compressed into intimate space
- bureaucracy colliding with bodily reality
- care work mixed with exploitation
- hospitality masking surveillance
- magical infrastructure patched together just well enough to remain profitable

If the city is elegant hypocrisy at large scale, the bathhouse is that hypocrisy made damp, noisy, and unavoidable.

### Procedural Design Hooks
This setting role supports concrete runtime systems.

#### Scenario Templates
The bathhouse can host scenarios rooted in its function:

- injury coverup in a treatment room
- potion resale through staff service routes
- insurance fraud tied to damaged fixtures
- pack dispute spilling into customer space
- stolen conveyance hidden through supplier access

#### NPC Relationship Roles
Roles read clearly in this environment:

- manager protecting the business
- worker who saw too much
- patron who owes money
- regular who keeps returning to the same dangerous person
- supplier or fixer using the bathhouse as neutral ground

#### Clue Families
The location naturally supports:

- towels, robes, and laundry tags
- intake forms and waiver records
- locker tokens and key bands
- maintenance tickets
- overheard gossip in steam-obscured spaces
- ward faults and fixture logs
- traces of blood, fur, ash, glamour residue, or contaminated water

### Prototype Guidance
For the first playable slice, the bathhouse only needs enough outside-world pressure to make the mystery legible.

The vertical slice should prove:

- this is a real business with operating constraints
- each key NPC has a believable reason to be here
- the central liability can connect to everyone without feeling contrived
- clues arise from social and institutional behavior, not only monster tells

### Relationship To Existing Docs
This document should be read alongside the broader setting note and the newer Codex brief.

Where older prototype docs say the core question is "which named NPC is secretly the werewolf," treat that as an outdated prototype framing.

The stronger direction is:

- werewolves are normal in the setting
- one specific werewolf or werewolf-adjacent liability is causing the run's crisis
- many NPCs look suspicious because they are entangled in the fallout

That gives the project a much more distinctive spine than a plain hidden-monster shuffle.
