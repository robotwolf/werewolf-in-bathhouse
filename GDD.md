# Werewolf Bathhouse

## Prototype Game Design Document

### High Concept
*Werewolf Bathhouse* is a first-person horror-comedy social mystery set in a grimy urban bathhouse on the service edge of a wealthy magical city. The player works the night shift, keeps the bathhouse running, and investigates a growing institutional mess centered on one reckless werewolf whose behavior is threatening the business, the Boss's authority, and everyone tied to him.

The key twist is structural, not cosmetic:

**The main secret is not that werewolves exist.**

Werewolves are ordinary people in this world. The crisis is that one specific werewolf is sloppy, violent, politically inconvenient, and bad for business.

The tone is a straight-faced blend of dread, absurdity, and social embarrassment. Horror comes from uncertainty, steam-obscured danger, and loss of control. Comedy comes from bad judgment, workplace normalization of insanity, and suspicious adults trying to preserve their dignity while the whole place slides sideways.

### Player Fantasy
The player is the only person in the building practical enough to keep the bathhouse functioning and curious enough to realize that everyone's version of events is evasive in a different way.

You are not a chosen hero.
You are a worker who becomes useful because you can read the room.

### Core Pillars
#### 1. Social Deduction
The player studies named NPCs, compares contradictions, gathers clues, and works out the web of secrets around the central liability.

#### 2. Environmental Uncertainty
Steam, reflections, wet surfaces, muffled sound, and shifting architecture make perception unreliable without making the space unreadable.

#### 3. Crisis Management
The player is not just solving a mystery. They must also do their job, maintain bathhouse operations, calm patrons, and keep the building from tipping into total failure.

#### 4. Institutional Horror
The setting's menace comes from systems as much as monsters: licenses, insurance, pack authority, hidden obligations, and management solving disasters in ways that are entirely normal for this world and deeply alarming to the player.

### Core Gameplay Loop
The player loops through five activities during a run:

1. Receive or choose a maintenance task that takes them to a part of the bathhouse.
2. Observe NPC behavior and search for clues while completing that task.
3. Interpret evidence that may be true, misleading, planted, or self-protective.
4. Decide who to question, trust, monitor, help, or quietly avoid.
5. React to a supernatural or social escalation that changes access, danger, or NPC behavior.

This loop repeats across the night as moon pressure rises, operations degrade, and the truth becomes clearer and more dangerous.

### Run Structure
Each run represents one night shift during a full-moon incident.

#### Phase 1: Opening Hours
- The bathhouse layout is assembled from authored room modules.
- Key NPCs arrive with stable identities, current-run secrets, and routine behaviors.
- The player learns the shift rhythm while handling normal tasks.

#### Phase 2: First Signs
- Contradictions begin to emerge.
- Strange traces and awkward interactions create multiple suspect theories.
- The player starts realizing several people are hiding ties to the same problem.

#### Phase 3: Pressure Build
- The central liability becomes more active, reckless, or visible.
- NPC routines become less stable as stress rises.
- Clue chains reveal that the issue is not species identity but a dense social mess around one dangerous person.

#### Phase 4: Moonrise Event
- The bathhouse partially reconfigures.
- One route closes and another opens.
- Steam, sound, and movement patterns become more threatening.
- Some NPCs panic, hide, cover their tracks, or attempt damage control.

#### Phase 5: Confrontation And Resolution
- The player either identifies the correct liability and hands the problem upward, misreads the situation and causes fallout, or survives without certainty.
- A correct route can end in a direct endgame handoff to the Boss, revealing the larger power structure behind the bathhouse.

### Setting Frame
The game takes place on the service outskirts of a wealthy magical city whose official self-image is cultured, regulated, and refined.

The bathhouse exists in the city's grim practical belt:

- utility tunnels
- service depots
- worker housing
- low-rent magical commerce
- nightlife tolerated by inspectors who prefer not to stay late
- businesses that the respectable districts rely on but pretend not to see

The city is not secretly magical.
It is openly magical, bureaucratically magical, and unevenly humane.

### World Rules
The setting should follow a few stable rules.

#### Werewolves Are Normal
Lycanthropy is socially meaningful, legally regulated, and culturally present, but not an automatic reveal.

#### Magic Is Infrastructure
The world runs on magical plumbing, magical maintenance, magical labor, and magical paperwork.

Examples:
- badge sprites for staff access
- goblin service boxes for deliveries and requests
- enchanted boilers and wards
- glamours used like cosmetic patchwork

#### Institutions Matter
Power comes from permits, insurance, property control, pack authority, leverage, and reputation.

#### Respectability Is Performance
The wealthy city center exports risk to its edges while pretending to be more civilized than the districts doing the dirty work.

### Player Role
For the prototype, the player is a night-shift bathhouse employee with towel, locker, and maintenance responsibilities.

This role supports:
- access to public and staff spaces
- believable reasons to travel through the whole bathhouse
- constant interaction with people, mess, fixtures, and misplaced items
- a grounded perspective inside an absurd institution

### What The Player Is Actually Solving
At first, the player should think the question is:

**Which of these people is the werewolf problem?**

By the end, the player should understand the better question is:

**What is Ronin doing, who is tied to him, and how bad is the fallout if the Boss loses control of him?**

For the prototype, keeping Ronin as the fixed central liability is recommended. Replayability should come from the arrangement of secrets, relationships, clue locations, and suspicious behavior rather than from swapping the core troublemaker immediately.

Later versions can expand to a small curated pool of possible liabilities if the prototype proves the structure.

### NPC Design
The first prototype should use five important NPCs plus the Boss as an offsite authority until the endgame.

Each key NPC has:
- a stable archetype
- a public persona
- a species profile
- one run-specific secret
- one relationship to Ronin
- one suspicious surface behavior
- one clue trail or contradiction tied to that secret

Example prototype archetypes:
- the flirty regular
- the anxious first-timer
- the smug fitness obsessive
- the overly calm floor manager
- the occult scholar who swears he is only here to relax

The point is not to hide one literal monster among five innocents.
The point is to create five plausible knots in the same bad rope.

### Central Liability
Ronin is a reckless werewolf tied to multiple people in the bathhouse orbit.

He is dangerous because he is:
- impulsive
- violent under pressure
- socially entangled
- expensive to cover for
- a threat to licensing, insurance, and local authority

He may be linked to current-run scenario templates such as:
- insurance scam gone bad
- magical drug side business
- stolen conveyance chop job
- old jail connection resurfacing
- pack discipline problem spilling into public

### Procedural Mystery Structure
The bathhouse is semi-procedural, not fully random. The goal is replayability with coherence.

#### Authored Elements
- room modules
- key NPC identities
- Ronin as the central liability for the prototype
- scenario templates
- secret definitions
- clue types
- major moonrise rules

#### Procedural Elements
- room arrangement and corridor connectivity
- which optional rooms appear
- door lock states and restricted access
- NPC routine variation
- scenario template selection
- relationship role assignment
- run-specific secret assignment
- clue placement and truth state
- moonrise reconfiguration details

#### Procedural Layers
1. Select scenario template.
2. Assign each key NPC a relationship to Ronin.
3. Assign a compatible secret to each key NPC.
4. Assign a surface behavior mask under stress.
5. Generate clue bundles tied to room use, NPC movement, and current secrets.
6. Push the resulting truth state into the run.

#### Procedural Design Rules
- Every run must include all critical rooms and remain traversable.
- No run should produce nonsense combinations that destroy readability.
- At least one NPC besides Ronin must look suspicious for unrelated reasons.
- The player should not solve the run from one clue alone.
- The player should be able to build a convincing case through accumulation, contradiction, and pattern recognition.

### Clue System
Clues come in three categories.

#### Physical Clues
- fur on towels
- broken lockers
- blood in water runoff
- damaged fixtures
- dropped contraband
- maintenance evidence that something was hidden or cleaned badly

#### Social Clues
- contradictory statements
- awkward denials
- overheard gossip
- romantic tension
- financial pressure
- people refusing to name where they know Ronin from

#### Supernatural / Infrastructural Clues
- distorted reflections
- ward failures
- badge sprite records
- goblin service logs
- steam visions
- impossible sounds through walls

#### Clue Truth States
A clue may be:
- true
- misleading
- planted
- ambiguous

The player solves the mystery by building a socially and materially convincing picture, not by finding one magical proof object.

### Core Resources And Meters
#### Steam Density
Controls visibility and atmosphere and may reveal or hide evidence.

#### Suspicion
Tracks the player's read on the situation and may influence dialogue or risk-taking.

#### Moon Influence
Represents rising supernatural pressure. Higher moon influence pushes stronger manifestations, worse NPC behavior, and map instability.

#### Bathhouse Stability
Represents how well operations are being maintained. Low stability increases panic, locked spaces, management pressure, and environmental danger.

#### Task Load
Represents how much routine labor is piling up. Ignoring it should create both practical consequences and missed social opportunities.

### Setting And Room List
#### Required Rooms
- lobby / reception
- locker room
- steam room
- dry sauna
- showers
- hot pool
- cold plunge
- staff laundry
- boiler / maintenance room
- staff corridor

#### Optional Rooms
- lounge / juice bar
- massage hall
- private rental room
- roof access
- shrine niche
- hidden undercroft entrance

#### Endgame Space
- exterior service lot / parking area for the Boss handoff
- optional deeper ritual or infrastructure space for later expansion

### The Bathhouse As Institution
The bathhouse is not just a spooky location. It is a working business under pressure from:

- license risk
- insurance risk
- neighborhood reputation
- staff morale
- pack politics
- the practical cost of keeping ugly incidents off the books

Management keeps the place open because closure is also dangerous.
That decision is both darkly funny and dramatically useful.

### Tone And Horror/Comedy Balance
The game should take its absurd premise seriously.

Comedy comes from:
- bad social recovery
- vanity
- bureaucratic language used for impossible problems
- management acting like catastrophe is a staffing issue
- the player being forced to do normal work inside abnormal circumstances

Horror comes from:
- uncertainty
- steam-obscured threat
- social trust breaking down
- sudden violence
- the sense that the bathhouse and the city are both governed by systems the player only half understands

### The Bathhouse As An Active Force
The building may sit over older ritual infrastructure or semi-sentient systems that influence the night.

Possible functions:
- shifting room connections
- releasing steam at suspicious moments
- jamming or opening doors
- foregrounding some clues while hiding others
- reflecting stress back into the environment

For the prototype, this should be implied through moonrise reconfiguration and environmental behavior rather than fully explained.

### Endings
Prototype endings:

- Correct identification: the player correctly identifies Ronin, survives the interior endgame, and hands the problem off to the Boss.
- Incorrect accusation: the player blames the wrong person, worsening the situation and leaving the real liability active.
- Survival without certainty: the player reaches dawn or escapes but fails to resolve the crisis.

The correct route should support an "Alpha Intervention" style ending in which:
- the Boss arrives after the player's correct report
- Ronin turns openly hostile
- the player must route him outside
- the Boss reveals sanctioned pack authority through decisive restraint rather than a simple kill

### First Playable Scope
The first Unreal prototype should prove the game's core identity with the smallest viable system set.

#### Include
- 8 to 10 modular rooms
- 5 key NPCs
- 1 fixed central liability
- 3 scenario templates
- 1 secret per important NPC
- 3 clue categories
- 2 to 3 maintenance tasks
- 1 moonrise map reconfiguration event
- 1 final pursuit / handoff sequence
- 3 simple endings

#### Exclude
- combat-heavy systems
- multiple simultaneous liabilities
- large dialogue trees
- full city exploration
- giant lore codexes
- full campaign structure
- advanced inventory systems

### Prototype Success Criteria
The prototype is successful if players can:
- understand the bathhouse layout quickly enough to investigate
- notice distinct behaviors in named NPCs
- gather clues that support multiple reasonable theories before converging
- understand that many suspicious people are tied to the same mess
- feel tension when the map shifts mid-run
- make a meaningful call to the Boss
- want to replay because the social web can rearrange itself next run

### Technical Direction For Unreal 5.7
The prototype should favor simplicity and iteration speed.

#### Recommended Approach
- Build room modules as reusable Blueprint actors or level pieces.
- Use Data Assets for room definitions, NPC profiles, scenario templates, secret definitions, and clue data.
- Use Blueprint for most prototype logic.
- Use PCG mainly for dressing, prop variation, and spawn placement.
- Keep global truth in a run-level manager, not in every actor.

#### Visual Priorities
- volumetric fog
- wet reflective materials
- frosted glass and silhouette-friendly lighting
- readable but oppressive color contrast
- ambient audio that sells plumbing, steam, traffic, and institutional fatigue

### Open Questions For Later
- What is the final name of the Boss's Lycanthropic Pack Authority credential?
- How much direct conversation should the player have with NPCs?
- Should accusation happen through a formal report, a direct call, or a notebook-to-call flow?
- How much of the city's political geography needs naming before the player ever leaves the bathhouse?
- How much of Ronin's relationship to the Boss is stated versus implied?
